Chapter 1

The Crystal Structure of Solids

Donald A. Neamen · Semiconductor Physics & Devices: Basic Principles, 4th Edition · Pages 1–24

📋 1.0 — Preview

This text deals with the electrical properties and characteristics of semiconductor materials and devices. The electrical properties of solids are therefore of primary interest. The semiconductor is in general a single-crystal material. The electrical properties of a single-crystal material are determined not only by the chemical composition but also by the arrangement of atoms in the solid.

In this chapter, we will:

  • Describe three classifications of solids — amorphous, polycrystalline, and single crystal.
  • Discuss the concept of a unit cell.
  • Describe three simple crystal structures and determine the volume and surface density of atoms in each structure.
  • Describe the diamond crystal structure.
  • Briefly discuss several methods of forming single-crystal semiconductor materials.

1.1 — Semiconductor Materials

Semiconductors are a group of materials having conductivities between those of metals and insulators. Two general classifications of semiconductors are the elemental semiconductor materials, found in group IV of the periodic table, and the compound semiconductor materials, most of which are formed from special combinations of group III and group V elements.

Table 1.1 shows a portion of the periodic table in which the more common semiconductors are found, and Table 1.2 lists a number of semiconductor materials. The elemental semiconductors, such as silicon (Si) and germanium (Ge), are composed of single species of atoms. Silicon is by far the most common semiconductor material, owing to its abundance, relatively low cost, and well-established processing technologies.

Compound semiconductors include materials such as gallium arsenide (GaAs), indium phosphide (InP), gallium nitride (GaN), and many others. These materials have special properties that make them suitable for optoelectronic devices (LEDs, lasers) and high-frequency applications.

📝 Key Point: Silicon is the dominant semiconductor material for integrated circuits, while compound semiconductors (especially III–V materials) are critical for optoelectronics and high-speed applications.

1.2 — Types of Solids

Solid materials can be classified into three general categories based on the arrangement of atoms:

1. Amorphous

An amorphous material has no recognizable long-range order in the atomic arrangement. The atoms are bonded in a relatively disordered structure. Examples include glass, plastics, and amorphous silicon (a-Si) used in thin-film solar cells and LCD displays.

2. Polycrystalline

A polycrystalline material consists of many small single-crystal regions, called grains, separated by grain boundaries. Within each grain, the atoms are arranged in a periodic pattern, but the orientation varies from grain to grain. Most metals and ceramics are polycrystalline.

3. Single Crystal

A single-crystal material has a highly regular and periodic arrangement of atoms throughout the entire volume. Semiconductor devices are almost exclusively fabricated using single-crystal material to ensure uniform and predictable electrical characteristics. The high degree of perfection in single-crystal semiconductors is essential for modern device performance.

1.3 — Space Lattices

Our primary emphasis in this text will be on the single-crystal material with its regular geometric periodicity in the atomic arrangement. A representative unit, or a group of atoms, is repeated at regular intervals in each of the three dimensions to form the single crystal. The periodic arrangement of atoms in the crystal is called the lattice.

1.3.1 — Primitive and Unit Cell

We can represent a particular atomic array by a dot that is called a lattice point. Figure 1.2 shows an infinite two-dimensional array of lattice points. The simplest means of repeating an atomic array is by translation. Each lattice point can be translated a distance $a_1$ in one direction and a distance $b_1$ in a second noncolinear direction to generate the two-dimensional lattice. A third noncolinear translation will produce the three-dimensional lattice. The translation directions need not be perpendicular.

Since the three-dimensional lattice is a periodic repetition of a group of atoms, we do not need to consider the entire lattice, but only a fundamental unit that is being repeated. A unit cell is a small volume of the crystal that can be used to reproduce the entire crystal. A unit cell is not a unique entity. Figure 1.3 shows several possible unit cells in a two-dimensional lattice.

A primitive cell is the smallest unit cell that can be repeated to form the lattice. In many cases, it is more convenient to use a unit cell that is not a primitive cell. Unit cells may be chosen that have orthogonal sides, for example, whereas the sides of a primitive cell may be nonorthogonal.

A generalized three-dimensional unit cell is shown in Figure 1.4. The relationship between this cell and the lattice is characterized by three vectors $\vec{a}$, $\vec{b}$, and $\vec{c}$, which need not be perpendicular and which may or may not be equal in length. Every equivalent lattice point in the three-dimensional crystal can be found using the vector:

$$\vec{r} = p\vec{a} + q\vec{b} + s\vec{c} \tag{1.1}$$ (1.1)

where $p$, $q$, and $s$ are integers. Since the location of the origin is arbitrary, we will let $p$, $q$, and $s$ be positive integers for simplicity. The magnitudes of the vectors $\vec{a}$, $\vec{b}$, and $\vec{c}$ are the lattice constants of the unit cell.

1.3.2 — Basic Crystal Structures

Before we discuss the semiconductor crystal, let us consider three crystal structures and determine some of the basic characteristics of these crystals. Figure 1.5 shows the simple cubic (sc), body-centered cubic (bcc), and face-centered cubic (fcc) structures. For these simple structures, we may choose unit cells such that the general vectors $\vec{a}$, $\vec{b}$, and $\vec{c}$ are perpendicular to each other and the lengths are equal. The lattice constant of each unit cell in Figure 1.5 is designated as "$a$."

  • Simple cubic (sc) — has an atom located at each corner.
  • Body-centered cubic (bcc) — has an additional atom at the center of the cube.
  • Face-centered cubic (fcc) — has additional atoms on each face plane.

By knowing the crystal structure of a material and its lattice dimensions, we can determine several characteristics of the crystal. For example, we can determine the volume density of atoms.

📐 EXAMPLE 1.1 — Volume Density of Atoms

Objective: Find the volume density of atoms in a crystal.

Consider a single-crystal material that is a body-centered cubic (bcc), as shown in Figure 1.5b, with a lattice constant $a = 5\ \text{Å} = 5 \times 10^{-8}\ \text{cm}$. A corner atom is shared by eight unit cells that meet at each corner, so each corner atom effectively contributes one-eighth of its volume to each unit cell. The eight corner atoms then contribute an equivalent of one atom to the unit cell. Adding the body-centered atom gives an equivalent of two atoms per unit cell.

Solution:

The number of atoms per unit cell is: $\frac{1}{8} \times 8 + 1 = 2$

The volume density of atoms is:

$$\text{Volume Density} = \frac{\text{# atoms per unit cell}}{\text{volume of unit cell}} = \frac{2}{a^3} = \frac{2}{(5 \times 10^{-8})^3}$$

$$\text{Volume Density} = 1.6 \times 10^{22}\ \text{atoms/cm}^3$$

📝 EXERCISE Ex 1.1

The lattice constant of a face-centered cubic lattice is 4.25 Å. Determine the (a) effective number of atoms per unit cell and (b) volume density of atoms.

[Answer: (a) 4; (b) 5.21 × 10²² cm⁻³]

1.3.3 — Crystal Planes and Miller Indices

Since real crystals are not infinitely large, they eventually terminate at a surface. Semiconductor devices are fabricated at or near a surface, so the surface properties may influence the device characteristics. We would like to be able to describe these surfaces in terms of the lattice. Surfaces, or planes through the crystal, can be described by first considering the intercepts of the plane along the $\vec{a}$, $\vec{b}$, and $\vec{c}$ axes used to describe the lattice.

📐 EXAMPLE 1.2 — Describing a Crystal Plane

Objective: Describe the plane shown in Figure 1.6.

From Equation (1.1), the intercepts of the plane correspond to $p = 3$, $q = 2$, and $s = 1$. Write the reciprocals of the intercepts: $(\frac{1}{3}, \frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{1})$. Multiply by the lowest common denominator (6) to obtain $(2, 3, 6)$. The plane is then referred to as the (236) plane. The integers are referred to as the Miller indices. A general plane is written as the $(hkl)$ plane.

Comment: The same three Miller indices are obtained for any plane that is parallel to the one shown. Any parallel plane is entirely equivalent to any other.

Three planes that are commonly considered in a cubic crystal are shown in Figure 1.8:

  • (100) plane: Parallel to $\vec{b}$ and $\vec{c}$ axes → intercepts: $p = 1$, $q = \infty$, $s = \infty$ → Miller indices: $(1, 0, 0)$.
  • (110) plane: Intercepts: $p = 1$, $q = 1$, $s = \infty$ → Miller indices: $(1, 1, 0)$.
  • (111) plane: Intercepts: $p = 1$, $q = 1$, $s = 1$ → Miller indices: $(1, 1, 1)$.

One advantage to taking the reciprocal of the intercepts is that the use of infinity is avoided. By translating the origin to another equivalent lattice point, we can also avoid the use of infinity in the set of Miller indices for planes passing through the origin.

For the simple cubic, bcc, and fcc structures, there is a high degree of symmetry. The axes can be rotated by 90° in each of the three dimensions. Each face plane of the cubic structure shown in Figure 1.8a is entirely equivalent. These planes are grouped together and referred to as the {100} set of planes (using curly braces).

One characteristic of a crystal that can be determined is the distance between nearest equivalent parallel planes. Another characteristic is the surface concentration of atoms — the number per square centimeter (#/cm²) that are cut by a particular plane. The surface density of atoms may be important in determining how another material, such as an insulator, will "fit" on the surface of a semiconductor.

1.3.4 — Directions in Crystals

In addition to describing crystal planes, we often need to describe directions in a crystal. Directions are specified by the components of a vector in that direction. For example, the body diagonal in a simple cubic lattice is composed of vector components 1, 1, 1. The body diagonal is then described as the [111] direction. Square brackets are used to designate direction, as distinct from the parentheses used for crystal planes.

The three basic directions and the associated crystal planes for the simple cubic structure are shown in Figure 1.10. Note that in the simple cubic lattices, the $[hkl]$ direction is perpendicular to the $(hkl)$ plane. This perpendicularity may not be true in noncubic lattices.

1.4 — The Diamond Structure

As already stated, silicon is the most common semiconductor material. Silicon is referred to as a group IV element and has a diamond crystal structure. Germanium is also a group IV element and has the same diamond structure. A unit cell of the diamond structure, shown in Figure 1.11, is more complicated than the simple cubic structures considered earlier.

We may begin to understand the diamond lattice by considering the tetrahedral structure shown in Figure 1.12. This structure is basically a body-centered cubic with four of the corner atoms missing. Every atom in the tetrahedral structure has four nearest neighbors, and it is this structure that is the basic building block of the diamond lattice.

There are several ways to visualize the diamond structure. Figure 1.13 shows two body-centered cubic (tetrahedral) structures diagonally adjacent to each other. The open circles represent atoms in the lattice that are generated when the structure is translated to the right or left by one lattice constant $a$. An important characteristic of the diamond lattice is that any atom within the diamond structure will have four nearest neighboring atoms.

The diamond structure refers to the particular lattice in which all atoms are of the same species, such as silicon or germanium. The zincblende (sphalerite) structure differs from the diamond structure only in that there are two different types of atoms in the lattice. Compound semiconductors, such as gallium arsenide (GaAs), have the zincblende structure shown in Figure 1.14.

The important feature of both the diamond and zincblende structures is that the atoms are joined together to form a tetrahedron. Figure 1.15 shows the basic tetrahedral structure of GaAs in which each Ga atom has four nearest As neighbors and each As atom has four nearest Ga neighbors. This figure also begins to show the interpenetration of two sublattices that can be used to generate the diamond or zincblende lattice.

1.5 — Atomic Bonding

We have been considering various single-crystal structures. The question arises as to why one particular crystal structure is favored over another for a particular assembly of atoms. A fundamental law of nature is that the total energy of a system in thermal equilibrium tends to reach a minimum value. The interaction that occurs between atoms to form a solid and to reach the minimum total energy depends on the type of atom or atoms involved.

The interaction between atoms can be described by quantum mechanics. Although an introduction to quantum mechanics is presented in the next chapter, we can nevertheless obtain a qualitative understanding of how various atoms interact by considering the valence (outermost) electrons of an atom. Four major types of atomic bonding are discussed:

1. Ionic Bonding

Atoms at the two extremes of the periodic table (excepting the inert elements) tend to lose or gain valence electrons, thus forming ions. Group I elements tend to lose their one electron and become positively charged, while group VII elements tend to gain an electron and become negatively charged. These oppositely charged ions then experience a Coulomb attraction and form a bond referred to as an ionic bond. If the ions get too close, a repulsive force becomes dominant, so an equilibrium distance results. A classic example is sodium chloride (NaCl).

2. Covalent Bonding

Another atomic bond that tends to achieve closed-valence energy shells is covalent bonding. A hydrogen atom has one electron and needs one more to complete the lowest energy shell. Covalent bonding results in electrons being shared between atoms, so that in effect the valence energy shell of each atom is full.

Atoms in group IV of the periodic table, such as silicon and germanium, also tend to form covalent bonds. Each of these elements has four valence electrons and needs four more to complete the valence energy shell. If a silicon atom has four nearest neighbors, with each neighbor atom contributing one valence electron to be shared, then the center atom will in effect have eight electrons in its outer shell.

The four nearest neighbors in silicon forming the covalent bond correspond to the tetrahedral structure and the diamond lattice. Atomic bonding and crystal structure are directly related.

3. Metallic Bonding

Group I elements have one valence electron. When multiple sodium atoms are brought into close proximity, the valence electrons interact in a way similar to covalent bonding. Solid sodium has a body-centered cubic structure, so each atom has eight nearest neighbors with each atom sharing many valence electrons. We may think of the positive metallic ions as being surrounded by a "sea" of negative electrons, the solid being held together by electrostatic forces.

4. Van der Waals Bonding

A fourth type of atomic bond, called the Van der Waals bond, is the weakest of the chemical bonds. An HF molecule, for example, is formed by an ionic bond. The effective center of positive charge is not the same as the effective center of negative charge, resulting in a small electric dipole that can interact with dipoles of other molecules. Solids formed by Van der Waals bonds have relatively low melting temperatures — most are in gaseous form at room temperature.

1.6 — Imperfections and Impurities in Solids

Up to this point, we have been considering an ideal single-crystal structure. In a real crystal, the lattice is not perfect, but contains imperfections or defects. Imperfections tend to alter the electrical properties of a material and, in some cases, electrical parameters can be dominated by these defects or impurities.

1.6.1 — Imperfections in Solids

Several types of imperfections exist in real crystals:

Lattice Vibrations (Phonons)

All crystals have atomic thermal vibration. The atoms in a crystal have a certain thermal energy, which is a function of temperature. This thermal energy causes the atoms to vibrate in a random manner about an equilibrium lattice point. This random thermal motion causes the distance between atoms to randomly fluctuate, slightly disrupting the perfect geometric arrangement. This imperfection, called lattice vibrations (or phonons), affects some electrical parameters such as carrier mobility.

Point Defects

Several types of point defects exist:

  • Vacancy: A missing atom at a lattice site — an empty lattice position where an atom should be.
  • Interstitial: An extra atom positioned between regular lattice sites.
  • Frenkel defect: A vacancy–interstitial pair — an atom moves from a lattice site to an interstitial position.
  • Substitutional impurity: A foreign atom occupying a regular lattice site (discussed further in 1.6.2).

Line Defects (Dislocations)

Dislocations are line defects in the crystal structure. An edge dislocation occurs when an extra half-plane of atoms is inserted into the crystal. A screw dislocation results from a shear distortion of the lattice. Dislocations can significantly affect the mechanical and electrical properties of semiconductor materials and can act as recombination centers for excess carriers.

1.6.2 — Impurities in Solids

Impurity atoms are foreign atoms present in the crystal. Some impurities are intentionally added to control the electrical properties of the semiconductor — this process is called doping. In silicon and germanium:

  • Donor impurities (Group V elements: P, As, Sb) — have five valence electrons. Four are used in covalent bonding; the fifth is loosely bound and can contribute to electrical conduction. These create n-type semiconductors.
  • Acceptor impurities (Group III elements: B, Al, Ga) — have three valence electrons. They "accept" an electron to complete the covalent bonds, creating a hole. These create p-type semiconductors.
⚡ Key Concept: The controlled introduction of impurities (doping) is the foundation of all semiconductor device technology. By selectively doping different regions of a semiconductor crystal, we can create p–n junctions, transistors, and integrated circuits.

1.7 — Growth of Semiconductor Materials

The formation, or growth, of single-crystal semiconductor material is an important part of semiconductor technology. The most common techniques are discussed below.

1.7.1 — Growth from a Melt (Czochralski Method)

The most common method for growing single-crystal silicon is the Czochralski (CZ) method. In this technique:

  1. High-purity polycrystalline silicon is placed in a quartz crucible and heated above the melting point (~1415°C for Si).
  2. A small seed crystal with the desired crystallographic orientation is lowered to touch the melt surface.
  3. The seed is slowly pulled upward while being rotated. As it is pulled, the molten silicon solidifies onto the seed, maintaining the same crystal orientation.
  4. The result is a large single-crystal ingot (typically 200–300 mm in diameter and over 1 meter long).
  5. The ingot is then sliced into thin wafers using diamond saws, polished, and used as substrates for device fabrication.

1.7.2 — Epitaxial Growth

Epitaxy is the process of growing a thin single-crystal layer on a single-crystal substrate. The word "epitaxy" comes from the Greek epi (upon) and taxis (arrangement). The epitaxial layer maintains the same crystal orientation as the substrate. Several epitaxial growth techniques are used:

Liquid Phase Epitaxy (LPE)

The substrate is brought into contact with a saturated solution of the material to be grown. As the solution cools, the material precipitates onto the substrate, forming an epitaxial layer. LPE is relatively simple but offers limited control over layer thickness and composition.

Vapor Phase Epitaxy (VPE) / Chemical Vapor Deposition (CVD)

Gaseous reactants are introduced into a reaction chamber containing the heated substrate. Chemical reactions at the substrate surface deposit the desired material. For silicon epitaxy, gases such as silane (SiH₄) or silicon tetrachloride (SiCl₄) are used with hydrogen at temperatures around 1000–1200°C:

$$\text{SiCl}_4 + 2\text{H}_2 \rightarrow \text{Si} + 4\text{HCl}$$

Molecular Beam Epitaxy (MBE)

MBE is an ultra-high-vacuum technique in which atomic or molecular beams are directed onto a heated substrate. The beams are generated by evaporating source materials in effusion cells. MBE offers atomic-layer precision and is widely used for compound semiconductor devices (GaAs, InP) and advanced structures such as quantum wells and superlattices.

📝 Comparison: CZ growth produces bulk crystal ingots for wafer manufacturing, while epitaxial techniques produce thin, high-quality layers with precisely controlled doping and composition — essential for modern device structures.

1.8 — Summary

The key concepts covered in this chapter are:

  1. Classification of solids: Amorphous (no long-range order), polycrystalline (many small crystal grains), and single-crystal (perfect periodic arrangement).
  2. Space lattices: A periodic arrangement of atoms described by lattice points. The unit cell is the fundamental repeating unit; the primitive cell is the smallest possible unit cell.
  3. Basic crystal structures: Simple cubic (sc), body-centered cubic (bcc), and face-centered cubic (fcc). Each has characteristic volume density and surface density of atoms.
  4. Miller indices $(hkl)$: Describe crystal planes by taking reciprocals of the intercepts with the $\vec{a}$, $\vec{b}$, $\vec{c}$ axes. Directions are specified using $[hkl]$ notation.
  5. Diamond structure: The crystal structure of silicon and germanium, characterized by tetrahedral bonding with four nearest neighbors per atom. The zincblende structure is similar but has two different atom types (e.g., GaAs).
  6. Atomic bonding: Ionic, covalent, metallic, and Van der Waals bonds determine crystal structure. Silicon forms covalent bonds in the tetrahedral/diamond configuration.
  7. Imperfections: Lattice vibrations (phonons), point defects (vacancies, interstitials), and line defects (dislocations) affect electrical properties.
  8. Impurities and doping: Donor atoms (Group V) create n-type semiconductors; acceptor atoms (Group III) create p-type semiconductors.
  9. Crystal growth: The Czochralski method grows bulk single-crystal ingots. Epitaxial techniques (LPE, CVD/VPE, MBE) grow thin layers with precise control.
🔗 Connection to Later Chapters: The crystal structure concepts from this chapter form the foundation for understanding energy band theory (Chapter 3), carrier transport (Chapter 5), and ultimately the operation of p–n junctions and transistors. The periodicity of the crystal lattice directly determines the allowed and forbidden energy bands that govern semiconductor behavior.
